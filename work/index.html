Clinical Trial Screening Discovery

2020

We've known for a while that our inability to support the screening phase of clinical trials was a product gap. To understand how to shape the requirements, I partnered up with the Science and Product teams to understand clinical trial site and participant needs.

TLDR

  • Problem Our project managers and clients have been using roundabout ways to execute certain functions that our product doesn't support
  • Solution Working with the Product and Science teams, we ran a series of user research including stakeholder interviews, surveys, and workshops

  • Team

  • Lead: Me
  • Science Researcher: Xinriu Jiang
  • Product Manager: Steve Kurien

    • Use Case Matrix

    We scraped information from the study protocols we have access to and compiled it into one spreadsheet, allowing us to shape the configurability for setting up a study in our system.

    Stakeholder Interviews & Surveys

    We interviewed the CEO, President, VP of Customer Success, VP of Project Management, a Director of Sales, and a Project Manager and surveyed our Site Advisory Board. The affinity map below shows what was discussed in those conversations/responses.

    We understood that supporting screening was table stakes functionality—it may not necessarily add marketable value to our product, it would save the cost/time of roundabout manual work our users have to perform in order for the system to function as needed.

    Site and Participant User Journey

    Based on the themes, we pieced together a user journey for both the participant and the site sides. We validated the journey and the experience scale with the Site Advisory Board in a workshop.

    User Story Mapping

    Project Management, Science, Product, and Design collectively came up with 49 user stories, leveraging the user journey to guide the discussion. The output was a set of all possible stories for participants and sites in the context of our platform.

    Project, Science, Product, and Design assigned a value for each of the user stories based on our aligned-understanding of the user journies — since Project Management and the Science team were our primary stakeholders, their assigned values were weighted higher.

    Case Management System

    2019

    At the request of a pharmaceutical client, I designed a feature for case managers for a clinical trial. I am currently working closely with our front-end and back-end engineers for the first build.

    TLDR

  • Problem For an E. coli vaccine trial, case managers need a way to follow up with participants who report symptoms of E. coli within a study population of 600-1000
  • Solution Create a case management tool to keep track of and update cases of E. coli infection within the study population
  • Gist A big challenge was wrapping my head around all the moving parts and trying to simplify the experience for users. But really, the bigger challenge was running between our client, our project manager, and our acting product manager to narrow down requirements and nail down the workflow as our startup adapts to working with clients.

    • Team

  • Product Management Mike Kaminsky
  • UX Design Me
  • UI Design Jewelia Kim
  • Development Joey Wu (Front-end), Kailash Kumar (Back-end)

    • Background

    This feature would be the most front-end involved of the custom features we had to build for the vaccine trial. I lead the design for all features of the end-to-end solution, including
    • 1. geofence creation feature for study admin
    • 2. a participant-facing chatbot (with location-based conditions)
    • 3. workflow for creating of new cases
    • 4. case feature for case managers - which is what we're talking about right now

    Research

    Interviews

    When we kicked off our project, I interviewed our Chief Deputy Science Officer and our Research Assistant who have had experience as case managers at prior jobs. The conversations helped me find a footing for what they were generally looking for.
    • • They preferred having more flexibility over having less room for error
    • • Due to regulatory reasons, user should not be able to "undo" actions
    • • Note taking has to be general and not tied to a specific state

    Userflow

    I synthesized the requirements that came in piecemeal and created this userflow for final approval from the client.

    Information architecture

    To make the cases easier to track, I created 4 different states to align to the question the case manager had to answer in the progression of the case. Depending on their answer to questions, the case would progress from "Open case", "Reported (symptoms)", "Suspected infection", and "Confirmed infection".

    Wireframes

    From the breakdown above, I had a good understanding of the most relevant information tied to case manager's actions. I designed the layout so that as the case manager reads from left to right, they can glance at what information they need and directly on the right, answer the question that they need to answer regarding the case.
    Items such as PII (Personally identifiable information) would remain at the top.

    Interface

    I worked off of a design system that UX designer, Jewelia Kim, had created before. For the new components, I derived the styling off of patterns and added them to the library.

    Development

    To better communicate the conditions around the different states to developers, I've created truth tables. So far, it's been a great process working closely with back-end and front-end engineers to talk through workarounds. The release date for this feature is January 2020.

    ResQ

    Opioid Addiction Recovery App

    2018 - 2019 | Datacubed Health

    I am proud to have led the design of this opioid recovery app from challenge submission to bring it to life as an MVP, in development now. It won 2nd place in the RWJF Opioid challenge, was shortlisted for the 2019 Interaction Awards, and received press recognition!

    TLDR

  • Problem Too many people are dying of opioid overdose, and most people don't know how to help loved ones suffering from addiction
  • Solution Using predictive modeling, Design an app that sends nudges support network to intervene when said individuals may be vulnerable to relapse
  • Gist When our company decided to put resources into realizing the app, we shifted toward making proper flows and conducting user interviews to make it a viable product. The findings from user interviews (along with considering realistic dev efforts) led us to overhaul the mobile experience.

    • Team

  • Research Science Tyler Burleigh PhD
  • Product Manager/Lead Design Me
  • Development Marina Hoashi (React Native), Karan Desai (Back-end)

    • Background

    ResQ began as a response to a challenge sponsored by the Robert Woods Johnson Foundation (RWJF) and was initially led by Datacubed research scientist Tyler Burleigh.
    "Can you create technology enabled tools to help support and connect individuals affected by opioid addiction?"
    Social support plays a key role in long-term recovery. Because Datacubed’s research platform already allows participants to pass their data to research admins, we reshaped the model to connect primary users (recovering addicts) with a support network of family and friends. The primary users would be able to log predictive factors using their mobile app, while the support users would be able to monitor the primary users data to know when they should intervene. To address concerns that many who want to help might not best know how, the desktop web app would also provide guidance on how to best encourage and support the primary user.

    What science says are the key predictors of relapse

    Prior studies about opioid addiction reveal key indicators which predict relapse. The most accurate predictors seem pretty obvious, like how much a person is craving and when a person last used. Others are less intuitive, e.g. a person’s tolerance for ambiguity (essentially how okay they are with uncertainty) and their aversion to risk.
    Many of these factors could be be self-reported, but we could objectively measure some of the more subtle factors by asking primary users to perform several tasks. We design + gamified each of these tasks (please also see "Cognitive games") to measure key predictors of relapse:
    • • Delayed discounting | Impulsivity
    • • Stroop | Susceptibility
    • • Levy probability | Risk/Ambiguity tolerance
    • • Likert survey | Craving, Boredom, Stress, Pain
    As our Research Scientist Tyler described it, the Levy task presents the participant with “a choice between a sure bet and a risky bet with the possibility of gaining or losing some amount of money/points.”

    In a Stroop trial, typically, a word “is matched to the font color of the word (e.g., blue), and on other trials they are mismatched (e.g., red) which creates two competing response tendencies (i.e., red and blue). When mismatched, participants are slower to respond and more likely to make errors, and individual differences in task performance are believed to be diagnostic of decline in cognitive control which underlies certain disorders.” Replacing some of the color words with words related to opioids or heroin (e.g. “dope”) would measure your susceptibility.

    Explorations

    My fellow UX designer, Jewelia Kim, and I rushed to get wireframes and some (admittedly) extremely-roughly-visual-designed screens out within a day. Here’s what ResQ 1.0 looked like:
    Once we were selected semi-finalists, we had some time to breathe and refined the designs for ResQ 2.0.

    Userflows

    We were then selected as finalists. I worked with Android developer John Gomez to make a semi-functioning version of the app.
    At this point, Datacubed made the decision to open up dev resources to make ResQ a real, publicly-available app. So I quickly backpedaled to userflows.

    Research

    Interviews

    As ResQ began to take form as a full-fledged app, developers echoed the same lingering concerns we had that were never fully addressed: “How can we be sure that this is something recovering opioid users want?” We hadn’t done enough to engage with our potential user base to complement our scientific insights—what a way to imply “we know what you need better than you do.”

    After some deliberation, I decided to get in touch with users on the Opiates Recovery sub-Reddit. I felt like it was the best, non-invasive way to reach that community of recovering opioid users. With the approval of the moderators, I solicited feedback for the app. I had no idea how immensely helpful it would be: two people graciously volunteered to speak more in depth about their personal experiences, and each of the feedback we received shaped the next iteration of ResQ.

    Findings

    Looking at the response from Reddit, we felt optimistic that our app was generally well-received:
    • • Commenters saw benefit in calendar app with a record of their past symptoms
    I spoke with one of the Reddit volunteers over the phone and got a deeper appreciation of the physical and emotional impact of recovering. I also learned about a few things we hadn’t really thought about before, like the phases of symptoms:
    • • Acute withdrawal symptoms (5-10 days): “You’re so sick you don’t care about anything”
    • • Post acute withdrawal symptoms (10+ days): “You’re bombarded with all those issues once you sober up”
    • • 30 day marker: “30 days is huge for a lot of people”
    • • Access to AA/NA was key in their recovery
    I emailed with a second volunteer from Reddit who gave direct feedback about the app:
    • • “Susceptibility” may trigger fatalistic tendencies among some—i.e. the app is telling me I’m susceptible so my inevitable relapse isn’t up to me
    • • Reiterated that physical symptoms are a huge part of recovery and would appreciate guidance on how to alleviate them
    I also attended a very informative community panel which included a doctor, distributing attorney, and community leaders in the Lower East Side:
    • • Most members of the community are pro harm-reduction and safe injection sites
    • • The Good Samaritan law protects bystanders when calling 911 about overdose

    MVP planning

    The user interviews drove us to take a drastically different approach to the interface. While that’s not always ideal, it did give us a fresh start to to think realistically about how it would be developed. Considering the user feedback and the fact that integrating support user capabilities would take a lot more back-end work we just didn’t have the bandwidth for, ResQ version 3 became a self-tracking recovery progress app to be developed in React Native.

    So based on the feedback we received, our actionable items:
    • • Move from a dashboard interface to a calendar interface to look at metrics over ~30 day period
    • • Add ability to record symptoms
    • • Scrap “Susceptibility” as a factor
    • • Support chat > Humi chatbot (one Reddit commenter loved Humi so we wanted to make him more integral)
    • • Deliver educational materials/resources (symptoms, AA/NA information, your rights, etc)

    MVP design

    As a self-tracking app, ResQ 3.0 looks vastly different from our original entry. I looked at my favorite menstrual cycle tracking app, Clue1, for inspiration, making some improvements on it (e.g. more seamless transition from week to month view). The calendar view presents a data visualization of the risk factors over time, letting users monitor their own recovery progress. Based on their responses to the factors, we present them a “Resilience score”:
    Each of the factors’ weight varies toward the Resilience score (e.g. “Craving” is a more accurate indicator of relapse than “Boredom”), and if the Resilience score was low, the app sends out a word of encouragement. We’re hoping that we can build on this experience once we have more data to better predict when relapse might happen.

    We also wanted to supplement the Likert scale answers with some of the adorable minion-esque characters (made by our visual designer, Ivan Martinez). But first, we had to figure out a way to quantify the emotions to map them out to 0-4 scale answers. Tyler sent out an internal survey and the results determined which facial expression we’d select for 0-4.
    And this is how we ended up with our final MVP, no longer a mere proof of concept.

    Next steps

    We plan to do additional user testing once we have a build, but here some features we already have in the backlog:
    • • Making it “smarter” so we can make predictions about withdrawal symptoms or relapse based on real world evidence
    • • Connecting users to local support programs like NA/AA based on location
    • • Support users capabilities

    Time Use Diary

    2018 | Datacubed Health

    For Y-Combinator Research’s Basic Income study, we built an open-ended interactive timeline for participants to record their activities. With a lot of opportunities for user testing, our design shaped the transfer of this data—from how we prompt the user to process their information for input to the output of quantifiable data for the science team at YCR. It was incredibly rewarding to create a sort of sandbox for users and observe their self-reported outcomes.

    TLDR

  • Problem Recording daily activities is incredibly tedious—as we found out from research
  • Solution Design an interactive and intuitive timeline-filling task, making sure that the list of activities was comprehensive
  • Gist We conducted usability research (via paper prototype, mobile app) and analyzed quantative data to ease the cognitive load of mapping actual activities (e.g. "Washing the dishes") to a category (e.g. "Household chores"). We sent out qualitative feedback surveys to learn how to better accommodate intuitive behaviors.

    • Team

  • Product Manager Mike Kaminsky
  • UX/UI Design Me
  • Development Marina Hoashi (React Native)

    • Background

    Y-Combinator Research asked us to build an app for their Basic Income study set to begin in 2019.
    "How does receiving a basic income affect the way people spend their time? Do people spend less time working and more time taking care of family members? Do people drop out of the workforce or work fewer hours and go to school or pursue lower-paying job that they enjoy more? Using state-of-the-art passive data collection, validated with time use surveys, we will examine how recipients of basic income spend their time."
    YCR needed to capture activity data of income recipients, grouped into general categories, e.g. “work”, “work-related activities” or “leisure.” The task was to be based off of American Time Use Survey—we knew going into it that we had to rebuild a notoriously tedious task.

    Explorations

    Location-dependent design

    Our first pass in the exploratory phase was wholly dependent on location data—we would make assumptions based on your location and time of day (we weren’t yet thinking through the technical feasibility of machine learning). For example, if we noticed that you were at Hale & Hearty at 12:15pm, we would assume that you were having lunch (categorized under “leisure”).
    Our science team voiced some concern with this design because it made it too easy for people to provide false data. Taking away user autonomy + lazy users = bad data.

    Location-dependent enhanced design

    We thought through other ways to use location data to make the task easier—specifically, by blocking out chunks based on location and narrowing down the list of activities.
    But it was only after these exercises (as these things go) that we found out that less than 50% percent of the pilot participants opted to share their location.

    Interface

    Location-enhancedOpen-ended Design

    We decided that a drag and drop interface would best suit the task. We were hopeful that the tangibility of the interaction would make it interesting for users while keeping the responses open-ended. For convenience, it was logical to keep both the activity list and the timeline exposed at all times.
    Based on the in-house paper prototype, we added some categories to a list we had created (based on a more-generalized list that YCR provided).

    Research

    Usability Test: Paper prototype

    Because we weren’t able to ease users’ cognitive load by other means, we had to ensure that all the items in the activities list were mutually exclusive + collectively exhaustive so that users wouldn’t have to think twice about how to mentally map their actual activity to a defined category.

    Using color blocks (people actually volunteered when they saw these) and a giant timeline to mimic the designs, I asked other employees at Datacubed to recall their activities yesterday and to reproduce it on the timeline.

    Usability test: Mobile prototype

    Problem: While testing the built version on iOS and Android devices around our shared office, we observed a lot of people getting frustrated while using Android devices. I think I’m explaining this correctly, but—React Native on Android isn’t good at discerning “dragging” in a scrollable area. Because the left side is vertically scrollable, dragging activities in any direction beside precisely horizontal felt lagged.

    Solution: teach users to tap on the item (which disables the scroll) and then move it out (which would allow them to move it however they wanted).

    Data analysis: YCR’s pilot group

    Silas Lee, our research intern, ran an analysis for completion time.
    • • The average person took 135.8 seconds (2.3 min) less the second time around. Users were faster the second time around (hopefully because they learned the behavior)!
    We also looked at the responses to improve both the data collection and user experience.
    • • In “Other” users wrote in their own responses if any categories were missing. We took notice of the recurring write-in answers and added them as to the list.
    • • Some users were conflating “driving” with “commuting” and placed "commuting" while traveling to and from non-work related activities (which YCR has clarified they do not want). So we added "Driving" to the list and made the distinction.

    Data analysis: Beta group

    Next, we deployed the Time Use Tracker and a feedback survey to our company’s Beta group, a cohort whose data we use to validate surveys and tasks.

    Interestingly (although not all that relevant), the people who found it the least “Easy to use” were between 35-44 (this age group tends to have the lowest retention rate, presumably because they tend to have the most responsibilities).

    In the feedback survey, we slotted in a question that would solicit more discrete responses: “Would you do this everyday?” 17 said they would, 22 said they wouldn’t. In reality, no user is asked to do this every day so expectations were low, but I hoped the question would provoke more extreme responses. Of the users who reported they would not use the Time Use Diary daily:
    • 59% of them found it too difficult to remember or just thought it was too tedious (while it was easy to use)
    • 27% of them were just not interested in logging their day
    • 14% thought it was too difficult
    Based on the detailed feedback of the 14% of that group, we updated the tutorial to clarify the instructions.

    Next steps

    We've explored several options we could pick up and deploy at next client’s request
    • • Run usability testing to catch pain points in the moment
    • • Tutorial tidbits on an as-needed basis
    • • Clarify requirements in the tutorial
    • • Teach both ways for users to stretch the time of activity

    Cognitive Games

    2017 - 2019

    I worked together with React Native developer Marina Hoashi and research scientist Tyler Burleigh PhD to transform cognitive tasks—which have traditionally been conducted on paper or desktop (often crudely copied from said paper version)—into mobile games. The three of us negotiated the middle ground in creating a task that’s feasible, enjoyable, and scientifically-validated. Visual assets are a combined effort between amazing visual designer Ivan Martinez and me.

    TLDR

  • Problem For research studies, our clients need a way to assess different cognitive capabilities of participants. However, compliance rates tend to be low because these cognitive tests are usually conducted in a physical setting.
  • Solution Mobile-friendly games and surveys based on widely-used cognitive tests

    • Team

  • Research Science Tyler Burleigh PhD, Maximo Prescott, Eric Reavis PhD, Silas Lee
  • UI/Interaction Design Me
  • Development Marina Hoashi (React Native)

    • Ongoing process

    We want to make sure the array of tasks are exciting on their own while also aesthetically cohesive as a library. We are currently building a design pattern for game mechanics—such as feedback, scoring, and streaks—based on what we want to incentivize. To push the design further, we attach a strong theme to each of them. These themes have become very important because they dictate both the visual assets and the animations.

    Iterations

    In order to scientifically validate tasks, we go through multiple iterations and test after each one. A lot of times, it's a matter of changing the timings, but they tend to have design consequences (e.g. Go/Nogo: We need to decrease the time the stimulus was on screen, but realistically, the sushi can't disappear before the user "grabs" it. Our solution: we cover the sushi with a lid). Testing for validation also gives me extra opportunities to ask usability questions.

    Game 1: Go/No-go

    The Go/No-go task test for impulsivity. It's simple: there are two sets of stimuli, and the player presses a button for the "positive" stimuli appears and does not respond when the "negative" stimuli appears. You can play with a more-traditional version here.

    The trick for this was to incentivize speed while upholding the goal to be accurate. Our solution: more points for faster responses. Here's the version we built (it's sushi-themed):

    Game 2: Flanker

    The Flanker task is where the player is presented with either a "congruent" trial—all arrows pointing in one direction—or an "incongruent" trial—the arrow in the middle is pointed the opposite direction. While testing the Flanker task, we wanted to see if there was a correlation between the shape of the ducks (geometric to organic) and the test results. Alas, we didn't find anything conclusive. The third duck gave us the best results (most interference effect = incongruent - congruent effects) so we stuck with him.

    Game 3: N-back

    IIn this task, the player is shown one item per trial, and they must try to remember a sequence of "N" items, from the one on-screen to "N" items back. While the "N" could be any number, 2 is standard (not too easy nor too difficult). The player answers "Match" if the currently displayed item is the same as the one they saw 2 items ago.

    We went with a parade theme to maintain the sense of sequence (the less parade-like version of N-back can be found here). Because the task requires hyperfocus, we integrated the feedback into the background—although in most other tasks, we use notification toasts for feedback.
    From tests with our Beta cohort (Datacube's cohort of randomly selected participants), we found that almost half the users were interpreting the instructions incorrectly (but in the same, incorrect way!). Some users were only answering every third item, presumably because they didn’t understand that each item required a response. We hadn't seen this when we tested in-house. This is the glaring visualization of the issue, investigated by Research Assistant Maximo Prescott.

    Game 4: Balloon effort

    The original Balloon Effort task is a effort-based decision-making task. The player is required to decide between two options, which differ in effort and reward amount. The "effort" refers to the amount of times the player must repeat a mundane exercise (i.e. pressing alternating buttons).

    In adapting this to mobile, we want to test out "swiping on a narrow path" as the mundane gesture. This has not yet been developed or tested.
